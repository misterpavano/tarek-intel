Pipeline & Approvals
Regulatory Policy / RDEP / Plausible Mechanism

FDA Launches Rare Disease Evidence Principles — "Plausible Mechanism" Pathway Rewrites the Approval Rulebook

The FDA released its Rare Disease Evidence Principles (RDEP) guidance, establishing the "Plausible Mechanism" framework for individualized therapies targeting ultra-rare diseases with fewer than 1,000 U.S. patients facing rapid deterioration with no adequate alternative. Therapies must specifically address the genetic defect in question, with sponsors working closely with FDA to define the required evidence before a pivotal trial begins. CDER Acting Director Tracy Beth Hoeg called it "a revolutionary advance in regulatory science" — the first formal framework in 25 years for diseases where traditional RCTs are structurally impossible to conduct.
What this means
This is the most consequential rare disease regulatory policy since the Orphan Drug Act. For sponsors developing therapies in diseases with sub-1,000 U.S. patient counts, the RDEP creates a pre-agreed evidentiary roadmap that does not demand RCT data impossible to generate. The framework's emphasis on early FDA engagement before pivotal trials begins fundamentally changes how medical affairs teams should approach regulatory strategy — the conversation with FDA starts much earlier, and science is the primary currency of that dialogue.
Your takeaway
Any client with a pipeline asset targeting a genetically defined rare disease under 1,000 U.S. patients should initiate RDEP eligibility assessment this quarter while the public comment window is open. The framework's reliance on well-characterized biomarkers as approval evidence means your client's biomarker development and HCP education programs are now co-equal regulatory strategy tools. Build the scientific infrastructure — natural history registries, KOL networks, biomarker validation programs — as if they will anchor the BLA, because under RDEP they may.

FDA Approval / Pediatric / Ultra-Rare Cardiovascular

Chiesi Wins FDA Approval of JUXTAPID for Children with HoFH — First Pediatric Label Expansion Since 2012

Chiesi Global Rare Diseases announced FDA approval of JUXTAPID (lomitapide) capsules for pediatric use in homozygous familial hypercholesterolemia (HoFH) for children 2 years of age and older — the first expansion of JUXTAPID's label since its original adult approval in 2012. HoFH affects approximately 1 in 250,000 to 1 in 360,000 individuals, causing dangerously elevated LDL-C from birth that drives severe atherosclerosis without aggressive early intervention. The Family Heart Foundation's CEO called it a critical step for "children who face extraordinary challenges from the moment they're diagnosed."
What this means
Pediatric label expansions in ultra-rare diseases shift the HCP engagement target entirely. The physicians managing HoFH in children are pediatric lipidologists, pediatric cardiologists, and clinical geneticists — not the adult cardiologists who prescribed lomitapide before. Chiesi is entering a new HCP segment requiring a distinct medical affairs strategy, different KOL relationships, and pediatric-specific patient identification through newborn screening programs and family cascade testing.
Your takeaway
For clients in rare metabolic or cardiovascular conditions, the HoFH pediatric approval models cascade screening as a patient identification strategy. HoFH patients are found through family cascade testing after a proband is diagnosed — each diagnosed child implicates parents and siblings. This family-first patient identification model applies directly to any autosomal dominant or codominant rare disease where the index case opens a diagnostic pathway to multiple family members. If your clients are not mapping cascade screening into their patient identification strategy, this is the case study to share.

Fast Track / FcRn Inhibition / Rare Autoimmune

J&J Nipocalimab Earns FDA Fast Track for Lupus — FcRn Platform Expands Across the Rare Autoimmune Spectrum

Johnson & Johnson announced FDA Fast Track designation for nipocalimab, its FcRn inhibitor, in systemic lupus erythematosus — a condition with significant rare disease overlap in organ-threatening lupus nephritis and refractory SLE subpopulations. Fast Track designation enables potential accelerated FDA review timelines. Nipocalimab has now demonstrated activity across NMOSD, myasthenia gravis, hemolytic disease of the fetus and newborn (Breakthrough designation), and SLE — making it the most multi-indication FcRn program in development and a competitive presence in nearly every rare autoimmune specialist community.
What this means
FcRn inhibition is establishing itself as the most versatile platform in rare autoimmune disease, with nipocalimab pursuing regulatory milestones across five indications simultaneously. The SLE Fast Track positions J&J's program in a large autoimmune market while rare disease anchors build clinical credibility. The multi-indication approach is increasingly the dominant rare autoimmune business model: establish a mechanism in a high-unmet-need rare condition, then leverage that credibility into adjacent larger populations.
Your takeaway
For clients in rare autoimmune indications — NMOSD, MG, pemphigus, rare ITP — nipocalimab's multi-indication march is the competitive threat to model now. FcRn inhibitors reduce pathogenic IgG broadly, making the list of theoretically addressable rare autoimmune conditions very long. Identify which of your clients' KOL communities will also be targeted by J&J's nipocalimab MSLs and begin deepening those relationships proactively before the competitive field teams arrive.

Acquisition / Pompe Disease / Rare Metabolic Consolidation

BioMarin Acquires Amicus Therapeutics — Year's Largest Rare Disease Deal Reshapes the Metabolic Landscape

BioMarin Pharmaceutical entered a definitive agreement to acquire Amicus Therapeutics, maker of Pombiliti and Opfolda (cipaglucosidase alfa with miglustat) for late-onset Pompe disease — a therapy generating $634M in 2025 revenue, up 17% year-over-year. The deal is expected to close in Q2 2026. BioMarin, targeting $3.325–$3.425B in 2026 revenue, adds a commercial rare disease franchise with strong growth trajectory and complementary rare metabolic disease infrastructure. The acquisition also follows BioMarin's withdrawal of its hemophilia gene therapy Roctavian with a $240M write-down — a reminder that rare disease portfolio risk management remains a core strategic challenge.
What this means
This deal signals that large specialty rare disease companies are entering a consolidation phase driven by commercial efficiency: two companies with overlapping rare metabolic disease commercial infrastructure — MSLs, patient communities, specialty pharmacy networks — achieve synergies by combining. For Pompe disease, the acquisition means patients now interact with a combined BioMarin-Amicus commercial and medical affairs organization, with near-term disruption risk as field teams are integrated. The pattern will repeat across rare disease subsectors as the sector matures.
Your takeaway
Post-merger rare disease medical affairs integration creates a critical vulnerability window for competitors. When two companies merge, KOL relationships, patient registry partnerships, and advocacy connections are disrupted as field teams reorganize. If any of your clients have programs adjacent to Pompe disease or rare lysosomal storage disorders, the 6–12 months post-close is the optimal window to deepen relationships in those communities before the merged BioMarin-Amicus team stabilizes. Move before the dust settles.

TCE approaches could address rare autoimmune diseases at the B-cell and T-cell level in ways current therapies cannot — particularly in conditions driven by pathogenic autoreactive lymphocyte populations where current immunosuppression is broad and insufficiently targeted.

Spinogenix SPG601 for Fragile X Syndrome Gets FRAXA Foundation PI Support — First-in-Class BK Modulator Enters Pivotal Phase

Spinogenix announced FRAXA Research Foundation support for principal investigators leading the Phase 2b trial of SPG601 for Fragile X Syndrome — the most common inherited cause of intellectual disability, affecting roughly 1 in 4,000 males. The program holds FDA Fast Track, Orphan Drug Designation (FDA and EMA), and FRAXA's institutional endorsement — a combination that addresses the enrollment skepticism that stalled earlier FXS trials driven by repeated mGluR5 and GABA-B drug failures in this patient population.
What this means
FRAXA's decision to actively support PI identification and recruitment reflects high-level scientific confidence in SPG601's mechanism. FRAXA has funded FXS research for three decades and does not associate its name with programs it views as unlikely to succeed. The BK channel modulation approach is mechanistically distinct from every prior FXS drug candidate that failed — that novelty, combined with FRAXA's credibility as PI convener, is likely to overcome the trial enrollment skepticism that stalled earlier FXS programs.
Your takeaway
For clients with rare neurogenetic disease programs, the SPG601-FRAXA partnership illustrates the highest-value patient advocacy engagement model: an organization that doesn't just fund the science but actively recruits the investigators who will run the trial. This requires years of relationship-building before it becomes possible. If your clients are in Phase 1 or early Phase 2 in rare neurogenetic conditions, the time to build those advocacy-PI bridge relationships is now — not when Phase 2b enrollment opens and you need 50 sites in six months.

Skyhawk SKY-0515 Qualifies for Australian TGA Provisional Approval — Small Molecule Gains Ground as Gene Therapy Faces FDA Friction

Skyhawk Therapeutics announced that Australia's TGA determined SKY-0515 meets eligibility for registration via the Provisional Approval Pathway — an accelerated route for serious conditions with preliminary clinical evidence. SKY-0515 is a small-molecule splicing modifier that reduces mutant huntingtin protein production in HD patients. The TGA determination arrives as uniQure's AMT-130 gene therapy faces an FDA demand for an additional Phase 3 trial, illuminating a divergence in how different HD modalities — and different regulators — are evaluating evidence sufficiency for rare CNS conditions.
What this means
Huntington's disease is approaching a multi-modality, multi-regulator landscape: small molecules, antisense oligonucleotides, and gene therapies are reaching different regulatory milestones in different markets at different times. Australia increasingly functions as a validation market for rare disease programs seeking real-world evidence ahead of US and EU submissions. SKY-0515's TGA eligibility opens a path to Australian provisional approval that could generate the real-world safety and biomarker data to support future FDA accelerated approval — a sequential regulatory strategy increasingly used in rare CNS disease.
Your takeaway
Huntington's disease is the best-studied model for how medical affairs must operate when multiple mechanisms compete for the same specialist community: patient stratification by biomarker (CAG repeat length, disease stage), mechanism differentiation conversations with KOLs, and patient registry co-investment become differentiating activities. CRISPR Base Editing / Progeria / Manufacturing Partnership

Progeria Research Foundation + Forge Biologics Partner on SamPro-2 — CRISPR Base Editing Targets the Single Mutation Behind Progeria

The Progeria Research Foundation (PRF) and Forge Biologics announced a manufacturing agreement for IND-enabling studies for SamPro-2 — a CRISPR base-editing gene therapy designed to permanently correct the single G-to-A base substitution in the lamin A gene that causes Hutchinson-Gilford Progeria Syndrome. The therapy uses AAV delivery developed by the Progeria Gene Team, led by Leslie Gordon, M.D., Ph.D. (co-founder of PRF), Dr. David Liu at the Broad Institute, and collaborators. Forge will provide process development, cGMP manufacturing, and regulatory consultation. Approximately 400 children worldwide are living with Progeria at any given time.
What this means
SamPro-2 is the most scientifically precise application of CRISPR base editing to a rare disease yet announced in a manufacturing partnership. Progeria's single-base-substitution etiology is ideal for base editing — a corrector that changes one nucleotide without double-strand DNA cleavage has a theoretical safety and durability advantage over conventional gene therapies. The Forge partnership brings industrial-scale manufacturing credibility to a program that has been academically led for decades, signaling that the program is moving from research to clinical development in earnest.
Your takeaway
PRF's willingness to invest in Forge manufacturing services for a disease affecting 400 children worldwide reflects the field's acceptance that ultra-rare gene therapy programs must be resourced as if they will succeed, even when the economics are unconventional. For your clients in academic-partnered rare disease gene therapy programs, this is the most current model for how nonprofit sponsors can partner with commercial manufacturing CMOs to credentialize their programs and attract future commercial interest. Monitor PRF milestones over the next 18 months as an indicator of how rapidly CRISPR base editing is advancing toward IND in ultra-rare disease.

Gene Therapy Setback / Huntington's / FDA Evidence Standards

FDA Demands Phase 3 for uniQure AMT-130 — Huntington's Gene Therapy Faces Regulatory Wall, Shares Fall 40%

FDA rejected uniQure's bid to seek accelerated approval for AMT-130, its AAV5-delivered miRNA gene therapy for Huntington's disease, without an additional Phase 3 study — sending shares down more than 40%. Despite compelling early biomarker signals in Phase 1/2 data, FDA concluded single-trial evidence does not meet the bar for accelerated approval. The Alliance for Regenerative Medicine and HD patient community responded with public pressure campaigns arguing FDA's position contradicts its positive rhetoric on gene and cell therapy. The decision arrives days after FDA published its RDEP ultra-rare disease guidance — a jarring juxtaposition.
What this means
The uniQure decision exposes the gap between FDA's regulatory rhetoric — the RDEP framework, gene therapy commitment — and its actual application of evidentiary standards at the BLA stage. FDA appears comfortable with flexible evidence for ultra-rare diseases where patient counts number in the dozens. But it is not yet prepared to extend that flexibility to diseases like HD where thousands of patients could receive a one-time, irreversible intervention before long-term safety data matures. This distinction matters enormously for how clients position gene therapy programs across the rare disease spectrum.
Your takeaway
The HD AMT-130 decision is a critical calibration tool for any client considering accelerated approval for a gene therapy in a rare but non-ultra-rare condition. Single-trial accelerated approval in gene therapy faces a significantly higher bar than in small molecules — particularly when the intervention is irreversible and the treated population numbers in the thousands. If your clients have gene therapy programs at the IND-BLA interface, a frank FDA alignment conversation referencing both the RDEP framework and the HD precedent simultaneously should happen before pivotal trial design is finalized.

Regulatory Science / RDEP / iPSC Evidence / Ultra-Rare

FDA's RDEP Changes How Ultra-Rare Evidence Is Built — Biomarkers, iPSC Assays, and N-of-1 Design Enter the Mainstream

Applied Clinical Trials provides a scientific analysis of the FDA's Rare Disease Evidence Principles framework, emphasizing that therapies addressing the genetic defect underlying a disease may now be approved based on plausible mechanism evidence plus biomarker endpoints rather than clinical outcomes from a conventional RCT. This approach — already demonstrated in the n-of-1 ASO story of Mila Makovec (Milasen) — now has formal regulatory scaffolding that could support induced pluripotent stem cell (iPSC)-derived evidence, natural history registry comparisons, and expert testimony from the global specialist community as components of a valid regulatory package for submission.
What this means
The RDEP framework turns evidence-building for ultra-rare diseases into a collaborative FDA-sponsor exercise conducted before pivotal trials begin. The science that counts is no longer exclusively RCT data: biomarker validation studies, iPSC-based functional assays, natural history registry data, and mechanistic evidence from molecular biology all become components of a potentially approvable evidentiary package. Research collaborations that build biomarker validation evidence are now directly on the regulatory critical path — meaning academic-commercial partnerships must be structured with regulatory endpoints in mind from the start.
Your takeaway
For clients in ultra-rare genetic diseases approaching pre-IND or early IND stage, the RDEP's public comment window is open now — a rare opportunity to shape the guidance before it finalizes. Medical affairs leadership should coordinate with regulatory affairs to submit comments that advocate for the specific evidence types most relevant to their pipeline, whether biomarker endpoints, iPSC validation data, or natural history comparators. Comments from credible clinical and scientific voices carry weight with FDA. If your clients have the right experts in their network, this is a high-leverage moment to use them.

Disease Awareness / MCT8 Deficiency / HCP + Caregiver Education

Egetis Launches Dual Educational Platforms for MCT8 Deficiency — The Caregiver-as-Diagnostician Model for Ultra-Rare Disease

Egetis Therapeutics launched two updated educational websites for MCT8 (monocarboxylate transporter 8) deficiency — separate platforms targeting physicians and caregivers for this ultra-rare X-linked neurodevelopmental disorder caused by SLC16A2 mutations that impair thyroid hormone transport across the blood-brain barrier. MCT8 deficiency (Allan-Herndon-Dudley Syndrome) affects approximately 1 in 70,000–100,000 males and causes severe intellectual disability, hypotonia, and thyrotoxicosis-related systemic effects. Most affected boys are diagnosed with "unspecified intellectual disability" or cerebral palsy for years before a genetic workup reveals the causative mutation.
What this means
Egetis's dual-channel education strategy — separate platforms for HCPs and caregivers — reflects a sophisticated patient identification approach in which caregivers who understand the specific MCT8 clinical picture become their child's most effective diagnosticians. When a specialist sees the condition once in a career, a well-informed caregiver who recognizes the phenotype and can articulate it to a clinician is more reliable than HCP education alone. This model has consistently outperformed single-channel HCP detailing in rare diseases with long diagnostic odysseys and low specialist awareness.
Your takeaway
For clients with ultra-rare pediatric diseases, evaluate whether your medical affairs education investment is appropriately split between HCP and caregiver channels. In diseases with a 5-plus year diagnostic odyssey, caregiver education yields faster patient identification returns than HCP detailing in many cases — the data consistently supports this pattern. The Egetis strategy makes it explicit and replicable. Consider auditing your clients' current rare disease HCP-to-caregiver education investment ratio against the diagnostic odyssey duration data for their specific indication.
